# 主観内容の隔離 — Captive insurance market in Japan

> 出典エントリー: `insurance/captive-insurance-japan-market.md`

## 推論 (from Why a Japanese group forms a captive)

- **Retain underwriting profit** that would otherwise become commercial-insurer margin ^[inferred]
- **Self-finance predictable losses** that the group can absorb on its own balance sheet ^[inferred]
- **Access reinsurance directly** at wholesale prices (instead of through a commercial insurer at retail) ^[inferred]
- **Capture investment income** on premium reserves ^[inferred]
- **Customise cover** for unusual or hard-to-place risks (cyber, product recall, environmental, supply chain) ^[inferred]
- **Centralise group-wide risk financing** for global subsidiaries ^[inferred]

## 推論 (from 2. The Japan onshore captive licence gap)

Unlike Bermuda, Singapore, Vermont (US), Hawaii, Guernsey, Cayman, Luxembourg, Hong Kong, and several other captive-friendly jurisdictions, **Japan does not maintain a dedicated captive insurance licence regime under the Insurance Business Act** ^[inferred]. The FSA's 損害保険会社免許 (non-life insurer licence) framework is calibrated for commercial insurers writing third-party risk and applies the full ESR / solvency / consumer-protection apparatus.
This means a Japanese group that wants a single-parent captive cannot easily incorporate one onshore — the FSA framework imposes commercial-insurer obligations (capital, governance, public-policy distribution, complaint-handling, Policyholders Protection Corporation membership) that make a single-parent captive structure economically unworkable in Japan. ^[inferred]
The structural consequence: **virtually every Japanese-group captive is incorporated offshore**, while *fronting* (Japan-located commercial insurer issues the policy to the Japanese subsidiary and cedes most of the risk to the offshore captive) is used to satisfy local-issuance requirements for Japan-located property and liability risks. ^[inferred]
| Bermuda | Yes — Class 1, 2, 3, 3A, 3B, 4 licences ^[extracted] | Many (historically the largest single jurisdiction for Japanese captives) ^[inferred] |
| Singapore | Yes — Captive licence under MAS ^[extracted] | Many (Asia-Pacific consolidation since 2000s) ^[inferred] |
| Hawaii | Yes — Captive licence ^[extracted] | Some (Japan corporates with US Pacific exposure) ^[inferred] |
| Guernsey | Yes — Captive licence ^[extracted] | Some (European exposure) ^[inferred] |
| Cayman | Yes — Captive licence under CIMA ^[extracted] | Some ^[inferred] |
| Luxembourg | Yes — Reinsurance captive licence ^[extracted] | Some (European exposure) ^[inferred] |
| Hong Kong | Yes — Captive licence ^[extracted] | Some ^[inferred] |
| **Japan** | **No** dedicated captive licence ^[inferred] | None onshore; ART uses offshore captives + onshore fronting ^[inferred] |
This gap has been raised periodically in policy dialogue — including in METI / FSA discussions about Japan as an international financial centre — but no captive licence regime has been formally introduced. ^[inferred]

## 推論 (from 4. Fronting and the Japan-located-risk problem)

Japanese law (and most jurisdictions' insurance law) requires that insurance covering Japan-located property and Japan-resident persons be issued by a Japan-licensed insurer ^[inferred]. A pure offshore captive cannot legally issue the local policy. The standard solution is **fronting**:
This structure preserves local-issuance compliance while letting the parent group retain economic risk. Fronting fees and FSA-mandated cession limits constrain the structure ^[inferred]. The fronting insurer takes on:
- credit risk to the offshore captive (will it pay claims to the front?) ^[inferred];
- regulatory and reputational risk to FSA ^[inferred];
- ongoing administrative burden for the fronted programme ^[inferred].
The 大手3社 and several foreign-affiliated Japan insurers operate fronting practices as a recurring revenue line ^[inferred].

## 推論 (from 5. Tax treatment in Japan)

| Premium deduction for Japanese parent | Allowed only if the captive constitutes a *bona fide* insurance arrangement (genuine risk transfer + risk distribution) ^[inferred] |
| Transfer pricing on premium | Premium must be at arm's length; NTA scrutiny can recharacterise excessive premium as profit shifting ^[inferred] |
| Controlled Foreign Company (CFC / "Tax Haven Tax") rules | Offshore captive earnings may be includable in Japanese parent's taxable income if the captive is in a low-tax jurisdiction and does not meet the active-business test ^[inferred] |
| Insurance Premium Tax | Japanese policies pay premium tax; offshore captive cedes do not directly attract the same charge but fronting structures are subject to scrutiny ^[inferred] |
| BEPS / Pillar Two | OECD minimum-tax framework reduces some of the historic tax-arbitrage attractiveness of low-tax captive domiciles ^[inferred] |
The combination of CFC rules and Pillar Two means a Japanese-parented captive is no longer primarily a tax structure — it is a *risk financing* structure with tax neutrality at best. This is consistent with where captive design has moved globally since the 2010s ^[inferred].

## 推論 (from 6. Alternative risk transfer (ART) — the wider toolkit)

| **Single-parent captive** | Group self-insures via owned captive | All major Japanese groups (offshore) ^[inferred] |
| **Group / association captive** | Industry pool self-insures | Less common in Japan ^[inferred] |
| **Protected cell company (PCC)** | Hosted cells in sponsor captive | Some Japan SME / mid-corporate use, offshore ^[inferred] |
| **Rent-a-captive (ROC)** | Rent a cell for entry-level captive | Some Japan mid-corporate use ^[inferred] |
| **Reinsurance captive (RTC)** | Captive reinsures fronting insurer | Standard for Japan-located risk ^[inferred] |
| **Finite reinsurance / structured reinsurance** | Multi-year smoothing of underwriting result | Used by Japanese insurers via Hannover Re, Swiss Re ^[inferred] — see [[insurance/foreign-reinsurer-japan-landscape]] |
| **Insurance-Linked Securities (ILS) / catastrophe bonds** | Capital markets absorb peak Japan EQ / typhoon risk | Several Japan-peril cat bonds outstanding; sponsored by [[non-life-insurers/munich-re-japan|Munich Re]], [[non-life-insurers/swiss-re-japan|Swiss Re]], 大手3社 ^[inferred] |
| **Industry-loss warranties (ILW)** | Trigger-based payment on industry-wide loss | Niche; Japan typhoon / EQ ILWs trade in London / Bermuda ^[inferred] |
| **Parametric insurance** | Trigger-based payment on objective parameter (wind speed, magnitude) | Public-sector parametric (Swiss Re Public Sector Solutions) ^[inferred] |
| **Sidecar** | Quota-share investment vehicle for reinsurer | Reinsurer-side instrument; some Japan exposure via parent reinsurers ^[inferred] |

## 推論 (from 7. ROC vs RTC — practical distinction)

| **ROC** | Rent-a-captive (Rent-Owned Cell) | Corporate "rents" a cell inside a sponsor's captive structure; sponsor handles regulatory / capital / governance | Entry-level for Japanese mid-corporates ^[inferred] |
| **RTC** | Reinsurance Transformer Captive | Captive owned by parent group that *reinsures* a fronting insurer; main route for Japan-located risk | Standard structure for Japan-located risk where local issuance is required ^[inferred] |
Some Japanese sources also use **ROC** for "Reinsurance-Owned Captive" — the terminology is not always consistent across markets. The functional distinction is between *renting* a structure (ROC, cell host) and *owning* a structure that reinsures a front (RTC). ^[inferred]

## 推論 (from 8. Why the onshore licence gap matters)

- **Lost financial-centre opportunity.** Bermuda, Singapore, and Cayman earn licensing fees, professional services revenue, and resident captive-management employment from a market segment Japan currently does not capture domestically. ^[inferred]
- **Reduced FSA visibility.** Japanese corporate group risk is held offshore, outside FSA's direct supervisory reach (the FSA sees the fronting cession, not the captive's full book). ^[inferred]
- **Higher set-up cost for Japan corporates.** Offshore captive set-up requires offshore counsel, regulator engagement, and captive managers; an onshore licence would reduce this. ^[inferred]
- **Channel reliance on 大手3社 fronting.** The 大手3社 ([[insurance/japan-nonlife-big-three]]) gain a fronting revenue stream that would partially shrink if onshore captives existed; this is a quiet incumbency benefit. ^[inferred]
- **Reinforcement of foreign-reinsurer panel concentration.** Offshore captives ultimately ride on global reinsurance and ILS capacity (Munich Re, Swiss Re, Hannover Re — see [[insurance/foreign-reinsurer-japan-landscape]]) and on Lloyd's specialty capacity (see [[insurance/lloyds-japan-syndicate-operating-model]]). An onshore captive regime would still depend on these outwards markets but would route them via FSA-supervised entities. ^[inferred]

## Counterpoints

- Specific captive ownership claims for named Japanese groups are widely repeated in captive-industry commentary but are rarely confirmed in group annual reports; the inferred lists in §3 are structural reading, not regulator-confirmed entity-level disclosure. ^[ambiguous]
- A Japanese onshore captive licence has been periodically discussed in policy circles, but the policy reasons for not introducing one (consumer-protection consistency, regulatory simplicity, level-playing-field with commercial insurers) are legitimate and reflect a deliberate FSA stance, not a regulatory oversight. ^[inferred]
- Captive analysis is asymmetric — fronting insurers know far more about a captive's economics than third parties. Outside parties (analysts, journalists, academics) work from structural reasoning, not from full data. ^[ambiguous]
