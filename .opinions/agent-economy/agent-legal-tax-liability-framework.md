# 主観内容の隔離 — AI agent legal and tax liability framework · who is liable, how is agent income taxed

> 出典エントリー: `agent-economy/agent-legal-tax-liability-framework.md`

## Counterpoints

- **Constructive personhood may emerge through default rules.** Even without formal legal personhood, repeated regulator decisions, courts treating AI agents as quasi-persons for procedural purposes (e.g., service of process via the deployer), and tax authorities creating "AI agent reporting categories" can de facto build personhood by accretion. This was the path corporate personhood took historically.
- **DAO and decentralized-agent edge cases break the deployer model.** When an agent runs without an identifiable deployer (e.g., on a decentralized inference network, controlled by a DAO), the principal-as-deployer frame breaks. No major jurisdiction has resolved this; the 2024-2026 US, UK, Japan, Singapore positions are silent or ambiguous on agentic DAOs.
- **Tax authorities can carve out an "AI agent income" category.** Italy and Spain have publicly debated levies on AI-generated activity; the EU's Digital Services Tax precedent shows that an "AI economy" tax category is politically tractable. A future change here would create a separate tax-subject treatment without conferring legal personhood.
- **EU AI Act enforcement is the variable to watch.** The Act's staged applicability means most enforcement starts in 2026-2027. Early enforcement decisions (especially the AI Office's general-purpose-AI rulings and member-state market-surveillance authority actions) will establish how broadly "deployer accountability" sweeps.
- **Bankruptcy of the deployer creates a hard edge.** When the deployer enters insolvency, the agent's outstanding commitments to counterparties become unsecured claims in the deployer's estate. See [[agent-economy/embedded-wallet-custody-bankruptcy-mechanics|custody bankruptcy mechanics]] for the wallet-side of the same problem.
- **Japan and Singapore may diverge from US / UK on AI-agent KYC.** Both jurisdictions have stricter perimeter discipline around financial-services intermediaries. A Japanese FSA decision that an AI agent autonomously executing transactions is itself an intermediary (and therefore requires a license held by some person) is a plausible regulatory move that does not require granting personhood.

## Open questions

- Does any G20 jurisdiction grant a limited legal personhood to AI agents during 2026-2028 (analogous to corporate personhood but narrower)?
- Does the OECD or a successor international body issue model rules for AI-agent tax treatment by 2028?
- Does the EU AI Office or a member-state market-surveillance authority issue a high-profile early enforcement action against a deployer for an AI-agent transactional failure?
- Does the FCA SMCR explicitly extend to senior-manager accountability for autonomous-AI-agent transactional failures?
- Does the Japan FSA issue formal guidance distinguishing AI as a tool vs. AI as an intermediary requiring its own license?
- Does Singapore's MAS Veritas framework graduate from principle-based guidance into binding rules during 2026-2027?
- Does a major insolvency of an agent-deploying firm produce the first contested case on whether agent-generated obligations are bankruptcy-remote from the deployer estate?
- Does the [[agent-economy/agent-actorship-debate|agent-actorship debate]] move from philosophy to law through any concrete legislative proposal in 2026-2028?
- Does the CARF / 1099-DA reporting infrastructure get extended to require wallet providers to report on agent-side flows separately from human-side flows?
- Does a future skill-marketplace operator (Claude Code skills, OpenAI GPTs, etc., see [[agent-economy/skill-market-monetization|skill market monetization]] and [[agent-economy/claude-code-extension-architecture|Claude Code extension architecture]]) become a quasi-regulated marketplace operator under existing financial-services or consumer-protection rules?
