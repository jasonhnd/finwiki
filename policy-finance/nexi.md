# NEXI (Nippon Export and Investment Insurance)

## TL;DR

NEXI is Japan's public trade and investment insurance institution. Its role is to cover risks in foreign transactions that commercial insurance does not cover, making it the insurance leg of Japan's export / overseas-investment support stack beside [[JapanFG/jbic|JBIC]] lending and commercial bank co-finance.

## Role map

- **Institution**: Nippon Export and Investment Insurance / 日本貿易保険.
- **Legal basis**: Trade and Investment Insurance Act.
- **Established**: 2017-04-01 as the current special corporation form.
- **Ownership / capital**: state-owned; official profile lists JPY 269.4 billion capital.
- **Function**: insurance for export, investment, loan, and overseas transaction risks that are hard for private insurers to absorb.

## Strategic read

NEXI is not a lender like [[JapanFG/jbic|JBIC]]. It changes the risk profile of overseas deals by wrapping political, payment, and country risks with public insurance. That makes it a multiplier for banks and exporters: megabanks can lend, trading companies can invest, and manufacturers can export with a state-backed risk absorber behind the deal.

For Japan policy-finance analysis, NEXI belongs in the same system map as JBIC, JICA, JOGMEC, and the OECD export-credit rules. It is especially relevant when Japanese firms enter countries where commercial insurance capacity is too thin or country risk is too high.

## Counterpoints

- NEXI reduces risk for Japanese exporters and lenders, but it can also concentrate public exposure to frontier-country, geopolitical, or infrastructure-project risk.
- Insurance support is different from direct subsidy; the economics depend on premium adequacy, claims, recoveries, and country-risk classification.
- Transaction-level details often require separate NEXI announcements or project documents.

## Open questions

- Which NEXI products are most used alongside [[JapanFG/jbic|JBIC]] loans.
- How NEXI's risk appetite changes under economic-security and supply-chain-security policy.
- Whether climate and energy-transition projects change the loss profile of trade insurance.
