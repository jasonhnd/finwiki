# OECD Arrangement on Officially Supported Export Credits

## TL;DR

The OECD Arrangement is the rulebook for officially supported export credits with repayment terms of two years or more. For Japan, it constrains and coordinates how [[JapanFG/jbic|JBIC]], [[policy-finance/nexi|NEXI]], and other official export-credit actors can support overseas projects without turning export finance into a subsidy race.

## Rule map

- **Forum**: OECD export credits work and the Participants to the Arrangement.
- **Scope**: officially supported export credits with repayment terms of two years or more.
- **Purpose**: maintain a level playing field by setting financial terms and good-governance disciplines.
- **Participants**: includes Japan, the EU, US, UK, Canada, Korea, Australia, New Zealand, Norway, Switzerland, and Türkiye.
- **Sector annexes**: climate, nuclear, aircraft, ships, and other sector-specific understandings can modify the general terms.

## Strategic read

The Arrangement is invisible infrastructure behind export finance. When Japanese agencies support overseas infrastructure, LNG, aircraft, shipping, climate, or strategic projects, the financial terms cannot be set only by domestic policy preference. They must fit the OECD export-credit disciplines.

That means the Arrangement shapes what [[JapanFG/jbic|JBIC]] can lend, what [[policy-finance/nexi|NEXI]] can insure, and how Japan competes with other export-credit countries. It is especially important when comparing Japan's official finance with China policy-bank finance, which does not sit inside the same OECD participant framework.

## Counterpoints

- The Arrangement is not a complete map of all state finance. It excludes many non-Arrangement products and non-participant support.
- It covers repayment terms of two years or more; shorter trade finance and purely commercial insurance can fall outside.
- Rules change: the OECD notes the current version and updates, so transaction analysis should check the applicable version date.

## Open questions

- Which Japanese overseas projects use Arrangement-compliant JBIC / NEXI support versus other public tools.
- How climate-sector flexibilities change Japan's official finance for energy transition projects.
- How OECD disciplines compete with non-OECD state finance in strategic infrastructure.
