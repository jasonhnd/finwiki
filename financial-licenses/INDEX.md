# financial-licenses — Japan legal / financial-license stack index

## Scope

`financial-licenses/` is the standalone top-level domain for Japan's **law, regulation, and financial-license stack**. It routes the cross-cutting legal layer: bank licenses, securities / FIEA registration, insurance regulation, funds transfer / prepaid / electronic-payment-agency registration, crypto-asset exchange registration, stablecoin / EPI regimes, lending / installment-sales law, and policy-finance special statutes. It holds **8 thematic license-stack member pages**, listed under [Domain Members](#domain-members).

Use this domain when the question is **"what license / legal regime makes this financial activity possible?"** rather than **"which company is this?"**

This is a navigation and research-control page, not legal advice. Any actual license conclusion requires checking against the latest FSA list, e-Gov law text, supervisory guidelines, and counsel / regulator confirmation.

## Disambiguation

- For **financial-group entity / operating-company** pages (the bank, securities firm, insurer, card / leasing company itself) use [[JapanFG/INDEX|JapanFG]]; this domain explains the license the entity holds, not the entity.
- For the **regulators, SROs and policy-finance institutions** that administer these licenses (FSA, BoJ, JSDA, JFC, DBJ, etc.) use [[financial-regulators/INDEX|financial-regulators]].
- For the **thematic policy-finance layer** (government-finance statutes and mandates as a theme) use [[policy-finance/INDEX|policy-finance]]; this domain links to it from the policy-finance lane below.

## License Stack Pattern

Japan financial products are usually not governed by one license. They are assembled from layers:

1. **Balance-sheet layer** — who holds deposits, securities, insurance liabilities, loan receivables, prepaid balances, or crypto assets.
2. **Instruction layer** — who receives customer instructions and passes them to the regulated balance-sheet holder.
3. **Distribution layer** — who markets, solicits, intermediates, or embeds the regulated product.
4. **Self-regulatory layer** — JSDA, JVCEA, industry associations, exchange rules, or internal control standards.
5. **AML / user-protection layer** — KYC, segregation, cold storage, complaint handling, dispute resolution, suitability, and disclosure.

This is why a fintech UX can look like a bank while the legal stack is split across a bank, electronic payment agency, funds-transfer operator, prepaid issuer, securities firm, or crypto-asset exchange operator.

Use this table when evaluating a product idea, target company, or M&A / partnership structure.

| If the service wants to... | First license question | Likely route |

## Source-of-Truth Checklist

Before promoting a legal / license page to `likely`, check these in order:

1. **FSA licensed-operator lists**: confirm the exact license category and as-of date.
2. **FSA law / guideline pages**: confirm whether the regime is under a law, supervisory guideline, cabinet office ordinance, or Q&A.
3. **e-Gov law text**: verify the current statutory term, not only a market nickname.
4. **Entity disclosure**: confirm the entity name, registration number, group relationship, and service scope from the operator's official page.
5. **Self-regulatory body**: for securities and crypto, verify JSDA / JVCEA membership or self-regulatory route where relevant.
6. **Negative finding**: if a registration is not found, record that as "not found in checked source as of date" rather than "unlicensed" unless the regulator states it.
7. **Scope boundary**: separate legal registration from product capability. A firm may have a license but not currently offer a specific product.
