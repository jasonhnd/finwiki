# State-Chartered Crypto Arbitrage · 2025 US Federal vs State Race

## TL;DR

US crypto firms in 2025-2026 face a **four-way regulatory arbitrage**: the federal **OCC trust bank charter** path (Bridge/Stripe pioneered), state-chartered **Wyoming SPDI** (Custodia, Kraken Financial), **Nevada digital asset bank charter** (2023-launched, slower uptake), and **New York BitLicense** (exodus accelerating in 2025-2026). The competitive dynamic is no longer "go national or stay state" — it is "pick the state whose regulator extends MTL-exemption and Fed-master-account ambition the farthest." Wyoming has won the speed-to-charter race; OCC remains the gold-standard for full FedWire access; New York has lost outflow to Texas, Wyoming, and Florida. The story for 2026 is whether the federal **OCC vs Fed Board** stand-off (Custodia case, Fed Master Account denial) collapses the state-charter advantage or extends it.

## Wyoming SPDI — the speed leader

The **Special Purpose Depository Institution (SPDI)** framework was enacted by Wyoming HB 74 (2019) and operationalized in 2020. It is a Wyoming-chartered bank that:

1. **Cannot make loans** in the traditional sense (deposit-funded lending is restricted).
2. **Must hold 100% reserves** against deposits.
3. **Is specifically designed for digital-asset custody and FX-grade fiduciary services**.
4. **Has statutory authority to issue stablecoins** under Wyoming Stable Token Act (2022).

### Custodia Bank — the test case

Custodia (founded by Caitlin Long, ex-Morgan Stanley) was granted SPDI status in 2020. The thesis: Wyoming SPDI + Federal Reserve master account = "digital-native bank with Fedwire access," undercutting OCC charter cost and timeline.

The Fed denied Custodia a master account in 2023. Custodia sued the Fed Board (Custodia Bank v. Board of Governors). District court ruled in 2024 that the Fed has discretionary authority to deny. Appeal pending into 2025-2026. The political read: **the Fed will not unilaterally grant state-chartered SPDIs Fed access** without explicit Congressional direction.

The legal read for 2026: **Wyoming SPDI without a Fed master account ≈ a high-quality state trust company**. It is a strong custodian and stablecoin issuer, but it cannot self-settle USD on Fedwire — it routes through a correspondent bank (often Cross River, Customers Bancorp, or a similar fintech-friendly counterparty).

### Kraken Financial — the operational case

Kraken Financial received Wyoming SPDI approval in 2020 and went into limited operations. It has historically been smaller than the Kraken exchange but provides:

- Wire transfers (via correspondent rails).
- Digital asset custody.
- Limited deposit/withdrawal services for Kraken exchange users.

Kraken Financial has not pursued Fed Master Account litigation as aggressively as Custodia, opting for the correspondent-bank workaround as the de facto path.

### Two Ocean Trust and others

Beyond Custodia and Kraken Financial, the Wyoming SPDI ecosystem includes Two Ocean Trust and several stealth-mode SPDIs. As of 2026, the Wyoming Banking Division register lists ~10 SPDI-chartered or in-process institutions. The state has become the **default fast-track jurisdiction** for digital-native bank charters — explicit competition to the OCC's 12-24 month timeline.

## Nevada — the second-mover digital asset bank charter

Nevada passed **AB 466 (2023)**, creating a digital asset bank charter framework explicitly modeled to compete with Wyoming SPDI. Key differentiation attempts:

1. **Larger initial capital requirements** ($5M+ vs Wyoming's tiered approach) → meant to signal "more institutional than Wyoming."
2. **More flexibility on lending** (vs SPDI's strict 100% reserve).
3. **Better statutory alignment with NV gaming regulation** — meant to capture iGaming + crypto crossover firms.

**Uptake reality 2025-2026**: Public records show < 5 approved charters as of mid-2026. Nevada has **not** displaced Wyoming as the preferred state-charter venue. Reasons:

- Wyoming's first-mover network effect (Caitlin Long evangelism, lawyer / consultant ecosystem).
- Nevada's gaming-regulator-shared framework added complexity rather than simplifying.
- Fed master account access is uncertain for **all** state-chartered digital banks regardless of state.

The Nevada framework remains a viable **backup option** for firms that have political-relationship friction with Wyoming (e.g. mining firms, gaming-adjacent crypto).

## New York BitLicense exit pressure

NYDFS pioneered **state-level crypto licensing** with the BitLicense (2015). For roughly a decade it was the closest thing to a US "crypto national license" — Coinbase, Gemini, Paxos, Circle, all built compliance stacks around it.

**The 2025-2026 exodus**:

1. **Kraken** announced 2023 withdrawal from New York operations; completed shutdown 2024.
2. **Bittrex** exited 2022, but this was pre-2025 baseline.
3. **Binance.US** lost BitLicense effectively after 2023 enforcement actions.
4. **Multiple stablecoin issuers** announced "BitLicense + Limited Purpose Trust" was a worse cost/benefit than OCC trust bank charter once Bridge / Anchorage proved the federal path (see [[fintech/occ-trust-bank-charter-federal-stablecoin-arbitrage|OCC trust bank charter]]).
5. **Circle** moved primary regulatory anchor to OCC application track (2024-2025) and de-emphasized NY-anchored DSP arrangements.

NYDFS's response has been to attempt regulatory modernization (proposed "Greenlist" updates, custody framework refinement) but the structural pressure is real: **a New York-only license is worse than a federal OCC charter for any firm with national ambitions, and worse than a Wyoming SPDI for any firm focused on speed and lower fixed cost.**

The 2025-2026 inference: NYDFS's BitLicense will retain a base of NY-resident-centric custodians (Paxos National Trust, Gemini Trust) but will not be the growth venue. Compare the parallel international pattern in [[fintech/jurisdiction-list-monetary-protectionism|jurisdiction list 通貨保護主義]] — NY is becoming a "high-stickiness incumbent state for crypto" rather than the inevitable national gateway it was in 2015-2020.

## Texas — the pragmatic regulator wins

Texas Department of Banking issued **2021 guidance** explicitly allowing state-chartered banks to provide digital asset custody and to use public blockchains for permissible activities. The 2023 follow-up clarified stablecoin custody and reserve operations.

Texas's **comparative advantage** is not a bespoke charter (unlike Wyoming SPDI) but a **traditional state trust company charter + crypto-friendly interpretive guidance**:

1. Use existing TX trust company framework.
2. Operate under TX DOB regulator who has signaled "we will not actively block."
3. Lower compliance theater than NYDFS, more institutional-grade than Wyoming SPDI for traditional custodians.
4. Pair with TX's stable energy / data center infrastructure for Bitcoin mining operators (Riot, Marathon, Core Scientific).

**2024-2025 wins**: Multiple crypto firms have publicly relocated HQ or compliance anchors to Texas. The state benefits from regulatory predictability + no state income tax + energy availability. TX does not give the regulatory **innovations** Wyoming offers, but it gives the **predictability** that institutional players value.

## OCC vs state regulator turf war

Throughout 2024-2025 a structural conflict has emerged:

1. **OCC** asserts (via Interpretive Letters 1170, 1172, 1174) that national bank charter holders can custody crypto, hold reserves for stablecoins, and use independent node verification networks. This **federal preemption** undercuts state regulators.
2. **State regulators** (especially NYDFS) have publicly contested OCC's interpretive reach. NYDFS sued OCC in earlier rounds (Lacewell v. OCC, 2019-2021) on adjacent fintech charter issues.
3. **Wyoming + Nevada** chose the opposite tack — rather than fight OCC, they built **state-specific charters that the OCC has not preempted** because they explicitly avoid the "national bank" footprint.
4. **The Fed Board** sits adjacent: by denying Custodia a master account, the Fed signaled it will not bless **state-chartered** digital banks into Fedwire without Congressional direction. This is the **OCC's structural advantage**: only the federal charter route has a clear (if politicized) path to Fed access.

**The 2026 inflection point**: GENIUS Act federal stablecoin statute is expected to clarify which charter paths support federal stablecoin issuance. If GENIUS Act §501 (see [[fintech/genius-act-501-denylist-mandate|GENIUS Act §501]]) is implemented to require federal-level oversight for "national stablecoin issuers," state SPDIs could be relegated to non-federal-issuance custody work — collapsing the state-charter advantage for the largest issuers but preserving it for niche custody / non-stablecoin business.

## Comparison vs federal OCC trust bank route

This is the head-to-head decision matrix for a 2026 crypto firm choosing a charter:

| Decision factor | OCC trust bank | Wyoming SPDI | Nevada DAB | NYDFS BitLicense | TX trust + guidance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Speed to charter | 12-24 mo | 6-18 mo | 9-18 mo | 24-36+ mo | 6-12 mo |
| Up-front capital | $5-20M | $2-10M tiered | $5M+ | $5M+ | $2-5M |
| MTL exemption (cross-state) | Yes (federal preemption) | Partial / contested | NV only | NY only | TX + reciprocity |
| Fedwire / Fed master account | Yes (with friction) | Denied to date (Custodia) | Untested | No | No |
| Stablecoin issuance authority | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes (via DSP) | Issuer-routed |
| Political durability across administrations | Medium-low (OCC director changes politicize) | Medium (WY one-party stable) | Medium | Low-medium (politicized) | High (TX one-party stable) |
| Best for | National stablecoin issuers, full-banking ambitions | Speed + custody + WY-friendly stablecoins | Backup to WY | NY-anchored incumbents | Pragmatic institutional custody |

The dominant strategy by firm type:

- **Stablecoin issuers with > $1B AUM** → OCC trust bank (Bridge model).
- **Mid-scale digital-asset custodians** → Wyoming SPDI for speed, plus a future OCC application as scale demands.
- **Exchange + custodian dual-business** → Wyoming SPDI + state MTL stack OR Texas trust + state MTL stack.
- **NY-resident-centric institutional services** → Stay with NYDFS BitLicense + Limited Purpose Trust.
- **Bitcoin mining + treasury** → Texas, leveraging energy + regulatory predictability.

## State by state — relative regulatory posture map (2026)

A non-exhaustive scan of state-level crypto regulator posture as of 2026-05:

| State | Posture | Notable structural features |
|---|---|---|
| Wyoming | Most permissive + most innovative | SPDI, Stable Token Act, DAO LLC framework, Custodia, Kraken Financial |
| Texas | Pragmatic | Trust company framework + favorable DOB guidance; mining incentives; no state income tax |
| Nevada | Innovation laggard | AB 466 digital asset bank charter exists but uptake slow; gaming-shared regulator complexity |
| Florida | Politically friendly, structurally underdeveloped | Governor advocacy for crypto; state framework not yet codified; FL OFR remains traditional |
| Tennessee | Quietly permissive | Stablecoin & blockchain task force; tax-advantaged incorporation |
| Arizona | Politically engaged | Bitcoin reserve discussions; smart-contract-recognizing legislation |
| New Hampshire | Crypto-libertarian-leaning | Governor signed bills recognizing crypto as exempt-money-transmission for some uses |
| Colorado | Crypto-tax-paying state | Accepts state-tax-payments in crypto via PayPal conversion; minimal regulatory innovation |
| Illinois | Caution + DFPR scrutiny | Active enforcement against unregistered issuers; mid-pack |
| California | DFPI active | Digital Financial Assets Law (effective 2025) creates CA crypto licensing parallel to BitLicense |
| New York | NYDFS scrutiny | BitLicense + Limited Purpose Trust regime; declining new entries |

California's **Digital Financial Assets Law (DFAL)** is the under-reported parallel to New York — it creates a CA-specific crypto license that, like BitLicense, increases compliance burden but unlike BitLicense was enacted in 2023 with a 2025 effective date specifically to retain CA-resident-centric oversight. The bigger structural reality: **two of the three largest US states by population (CA and NY) have created bespoke crypto licensing regimes that limit the federal-preemption arbitrage value of OCC charters** for retail-facing entities operating in those states.

## Multi-charter stacking — the emergent 2026 pattern

The 2024-2026 trend among the largest crypto firms is **not** to pick one charter — it is to **stack multiple charters strategically**:

| Firm | Charter stack as of 2026 |
|---|---|
| **Coinbase** | NYDFS BitLicense (Coinbase Custody Trust) + state-by-state MTLs + OCC application track |
| **Circle** | NYDFS BitLicense (Circle Internet Financial) + state MTL stack + OCC trust bank application |
| **Anchorage Digital** | OCC national trust bank (since 2021) + state custody charters |
| **Paxos** | NYDFS Limited Purpose Trust + OCC trust bank application track + SG MAS license |
| **Bridge / Stripe** | OCC trust bank conditional (2026) + state MTL stack |
| **Kraken** | Kraken Financial WY SPDI + state-by-state MTL stack + global VASP registrations |

The strategic logic: **federal OCC = stablecoin issuance + national reach + Fed master account aspiration; state charters = consumer-facing operations + state-resident compliance + jurisdictional optionality**. A firm with only an OCC charter cannot easily operate consumer crypto custody in CA or NY without DFAL/BitLicense respectively. A firm with only state charters cannot issue national-scale stablecoins.

The 2026 reference firm structure has at minimum:
1. One federal-tier charter or charter pipeline (OCC trust bank or OCC application).
2. NYDFS posture (BitLicense, Limited Purpose Trust, or explicit exit).
3. CA DFAL posture (license, exit, or non-CA-resident-only).
4. One innovation-state charter (WY SPDI, NV DAB, or equivalent).
5. State MTL coverage for ~48-50 states for residual transmission activity.

## The Fed Master Account question revisited

The Fed Master Account access question is the **single highest-stakes binary outcome** of the state-vs-federal split. If the Fed grants any state-chartered SPDI a master account before 2028, the entire competitive landscape shifts: state SPDIs become viable substitutes for OCC charters for many use cases. If the Fed continues to deny master accounts, the state-charter ceiling remains structurally lower.

Current public posture:

- Fed historically: master accounts granted to depository institutions with "appropriate access to credit and deposit insurance." Wyoming SPDIs do not have FDIC insurance.
- Fed 2023 Custodia ruling: discretionary denial upheld; Fed cited financial stability + crypto-specific concerns.
- Fed 2024-2025 institutional position: no shift; Custodia appeal pending.
- Political pressure: Wyoming Congressional delegation (Lummis especially) has pushed legislation requiring Fed master account review; not yet enacted.

The probability tree as of 2026:
- 60% probability: Fed continues denying state SPDIs master accounts through 2027-2028.
- 25% probability: Congressional action forces Fed to grant master accounts to specific chartered classes.
- 10% probability: Supreme Court reverses Custodia ruling, forcing Fed to articulate a non-discretionary rule.
- 5% probability: A Fed administration change leads to discretionary master account grants.

Each scenario has dramatically different implications for the state-vs-federal arbitrage map.

## Industrial bank / ILC charter — the third option

A small but structurally interesting third path: **industrial loan company (ILC) charters**, available in Utah, Nevada, and a few other states. ILC charters:

1. Are FDIC-insured (unlike SPDIs).
2. Can have a non-bank parent company (unlike national banks).
3. Have been historically used by Square / Block (Square Financial Services), Nelnet, Toyota Financial, etc.
4. Have been pursued by some crypto firms as an alternative to SPDI or OCC charter.

The ILC option has been less popular for crypto than SPDI because:
- ILC requires FDIC supervision + full bank-style examination.
- The non-bank-parent exception is politically contested (Wal-Mart's ILC application famously failed under banking-industry opposition).
- ILC does not give the **crypto-specific** statutory accommodations Wyoming SPDI does.

Square Financial Services (Block) remains the only crypto-adjacent ILC charter as of 2026. Whether Stripe, Coinbase, or Kraken pursue ILC charters is an open watchpoint.
