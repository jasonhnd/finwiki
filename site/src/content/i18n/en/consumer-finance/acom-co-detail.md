# Acom Operating Profile

## TL;DR

[[consumer-finance/acom|Acom Co., Ltd.]] (8572  / TSE STANDARD) is a major consumer-finance company that is an **[[megabanks/mufg|MUFG]] equity-method affiliate (with voting-rights ownership above 39.6%)**. This entry covers **operating-level depth** across business segments, earnings structure, guarantee business, overseas expansion, and regulatory response. Founded in Kobe in 1936-04  by Masao Kinoshita as "Maruito Gofukuten," it later shifted into consumer finance and was renamed "Acom" in 1978 , listed on the First Section of the TSE in 1996-10 , and became an equity-method affiliate through MUFG's TOB in 2008-10 . Acom's **4  strategic core segments** are: (a) loans (personal card loans, with the "Mujinkun" automated contract machine as the physical channel), (b) credit cards (AC Mastercard), (c) **credit guarantees** (bank-loan guarantees for Mitsubishi UFJ Bank and regional banks), and (d) **overseas finance** (including Thailand's Easy Buy PCL).

## 1. Parent-group relationship

- **MUFG stake**: [[megabanks/mufg|MUFG]] holds above 39.6% on a voting-rights basis (based on the 2024  period report, including voting rights + trust holdings)
- **Consolidation vs equity method**: within MUFG consolidated reporting, Acom is treated under the equity method; Acom itself remains an independently listed company disclosing to shareholders on TSE STANDARD 8572 
- **Corporate governance**: directors seconded from MUFG coexist with members of the founder Kinoshita family
- **Position within the MUFG group**: the consumer-finance business sits in MUFG's **Retail & Commercial Banking Business Group**
- **Transaction bank**: [[megabanks/mufg-bank|Mitsubishi UFJ Bank]] is the main bank and the key partner in bank-loan guarantee business

## 2. Business segment details

### 2.1  Loan business (unsecured personal loans)

- **Flagship products**: "Acom AC Mastercard" and "AC Card Loan"
- **Physical channel**: the **"Mujinkun" automated contract machine** has long been part of Acom's identity. After an era when more than 1,000  machines were deployed nationwide, the footprint has been shrinking as applications move online, though demand remains in regional cities.
- **Online channel**: instant screening and funding through the official Acom website and app
- **Customer base**: individuals (mainly in their 20 s to 50 s), homemakers, and the self-employed
- **Interest-rate range**: annualized 3.0%-18.0% (below the revised Money Lending Business Act cap of 20%)
- **Credit model**: a combination of historical data, behavioral data, and credit-bureau data from CIC / JICC
- **Regulatory boundary**: like [[consumer-finance/aiful|Aiful]], it sits under the Money Lending Business Act + revised Money Lending Business Act (total-volume regulation: within 1/3  of annual income)

### 2.2  Credit-card business

- **Product**: "AC Mastercard" (Mastercard brand)
- **Feature**: marketed around "same-day issuance"; revolving-payment only, with built-in cash-advance functionality
- **Merchant network**: global access through the Mastercard brand
- **Money Lending Business Act + Installment Sales Act**: the cash-advance portion is regulated under the Money Lending Business Act, while the shopping portion is under the Installment Sales Act (CIA)

### 2.3  Credit-guarantee business (strategic core)

- **Business model**: Acom guarantees card loans extended by banks to retail customers (bank lends to customer → Acom guarantees the bank)
- **Main guaranteed products**:
- **[[megabanks/mufg-bank|Mitsubishi UFJ Bank]] Bank Quick** (largest guarantee client and the core of the bank-loan guarantee business)
  - Card loans at regional and second-tier regional banks
- **Strategic significance**: a **on-balance / off-balance role-sharing model** between banks and consumer-finance firms — the bank lends using deposit funding at lower rates, while Acom performs credit judgment and bears the risk
- **Guaranteed balance**: Acom's consolidated guarantee balance is in the hundreds of billions to trillion-yen range (see official IR)

### 2.4  Overseas finance business

- **Easy Buy PCL in Thailand**: Acom subsidiary and a leading auto / consumer-durables installment-finance company in Thailand
- **Mobile / retail rollout**: unsecured personal loans and installment finance in Thailand
- **Southeast Asia expansion option**: room for further expansion into Indonesia, the Philippines, etc.

## 4. Regulatory response and compliance

- **Revised Money Lending Business Act (fully implemented in 2010 )**: cap rate 20% / total-volume regulation (1/3 of annual income) / collection restrictions — Acom is already fully compliant
- **Response to overpayment claims**: following the Supreme Court gray-zone interest-rate rulings in 2006-2010 , overpayment claims surged, leading to provisions and actual payouts. Claim volumes have more recently been declining.
- **AML**: continued strengthening of identity verification and transaction confirmation obligations under the Money Lending Business Act
- **Customer-oriented business conduct principles**: adaptation to the retail-finance spillover of the FSA's fiduciary-duty principles
- **Act on the Protection of Personal Information**: management of the use and third-party provision of personal credit information via CIC / JICC

## 6. Strategy and recent developments

- **Mobile / web-complete shift**: capital is being redirected from "Mujinkun" machines toward web applications and app screening
- **Expansion of MUFG bank-loan guarantees**: guarantee-fee revenue remains stable even in a low bank-rate environment, making this a strategic growth area
- **Expansion of Thailand Easy Buy**: capturing middle-class demand for auto and consumer-durables installment finance in Thailand, increasing the overseas profit share
- **Use of credit scoring**: integrating behavioral and mobile data into credit models
- **Coordination with MUFG retail strategy**: cross-sell through MUFG's retail customer base, including guarantee-loan channels

## 7. Risks and regulatory issues

- **Shrinkage of the domestic loan market**: structural decline in cash-advance demand as Japan shifts toward cashless payments and prepaid cards
- **Limits on incremental credit under total-volume regulation**: under the 1/3  annual-income cap, additional credit capacity for prime customers may plateau
- **Rising-rate scenario**: risk of margin compression from higher funding costs
- **Country risk in Thailand**: political, currency, and regulatory-change risk in Thailand
- **Long-tail overpayment refund risk**: even if claim counts fall, reserve-revision events can continue to arise
- **Fairness / explainability of AI credit models**: AI-driven credit judgment raises discrimination and explainability issues
