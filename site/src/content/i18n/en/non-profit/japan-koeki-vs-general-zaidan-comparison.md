# Public-Interest Foundation vs General Foundation vs General Association vs NPO Corporation — Japan non-profit / public-interest legal form comparison

## Wiki route

Read with [[non-profit/japan-general-foundation-establishment-requirements|Japan 一般財団法人 establishment requirements]] for the foundation-mechanics detail and [[non-profit/japan-research-grant-map-2026|Japan research grant map 2026]] for the downstream grant-eligibility question.

## TL;DR

Four legal forms commonly grouped together but governed by **three separate statute families** and **two different supervisory regimes**:

| Form | Common name | Statute | Supervisor / approver |
|---|---|---|---|
| Public-Interest Incorporated Foundation | Public-Interest Incorporated Foundation | Public-Interest Corporation Authorization Act (on top of Act on General Corporations) | Cabinet Office or Prefectural Governor (Public-Interest Authorization Committee) |
| General Incorporated Foundation | General Incorporated Foundation | Act on General Incorporated Associations and Foundations | Legal Affairs Bureau (registration only) |
| General Incorporated Association | General Incorporated Association | Act on General Incorporated Associations and Foundations | Legal Affairs Bureau (registration only) |
| NPO Corporation (Specified Non-Profit Activity Corporation) | Specified Non-Profit Corporation | NPO Act (Act to Promote Specified Non-Profit Activities) | Prefectural Governor or designated city |

Key splits:

- **Foundation vs Association** — a foundation is built around dedicated endowment property; an association is built around members. **Endowment vs membership.**
- **General vs Public-Interest** — General forms exist by registration; public-interest status is a separate licence on top, granted by a Cabinet Office committee, that unlocks donor-side tax deduction.
- **General forms vs NPO** — both are non-profits but governed by different statute families; NPO has narrower permitted activities (20 specified categories) but lower setup cost, while general forms have wider scope but a ¥3M minimum endowment for foundations.

## Choosing Between Foundation and Association

Foundations are governance-by-endowment: a defined pool of property is dedicated to a purpose, and councillors + directors + auditors administer it. There are no members with voting power. Suited to:

- Research grant-making funded by a settled endowment
- Long-horizon philanthropic missions with a single original donor
- Family or corporate philanthropy where the founder wants the purpose to outlive the founder

Associations are governance-by-membership: members vote at general meetings on directors and major decisions. Suited to:

- Trade associations, industry bodies, professional bodies
- Member-driven activities where the constituency itself defines direction
- Lower-capital starts where no founding endowment is available

The detailed mechanics of the general incorporated foundation path — required organs, councillor / director / auditor cross-prohibition rules, board meeting frequency requirements — are documented in [[non-profit/japan-general-foundation-establishment-requirements|Japan 一般財団法人 establishment requirements]].

## When Public-Interest Status Matters

Donor-side tax deduction is the asymmetric benefit. Without public-interest status (or authorized-NPO status for NPOs), donors generally cannot deduct contributions from personal income tax. This drives the choice in three patterns:

1. **Endowed start, no immediate donor base** → start as a general foundation, run 3-5 years to build track record, apply for public-interest authorization. This is the standard staged path.
2. **Membership-driven, member dues only** → a general association is sufficient; no need for public-interest status unless dues themselves need deductibility.
3. **Public-fundraising model, must accept tax-deductible donations from day one** → either go directly for public-interest status (slow, hard) or pursue authorized-NPO via provisional-authorization route (faster but narrower scope).

The public-interest authorization substantive bar is high: financial test (50% of expenditure on public-interest activities), public-interest test (23 enumerated categories), governance test (independent councillors, restrictions on related-party transactions, restrictions on remuneration), and asset test (restrictions on retained-asset levels). 2024-2025 reforms tightened related-party rules — councillors / directors / auditors with a special interest relationship (spouse, third-degree relatives) cannot exceed 1/3 of the board.

## NPO Corporation — Different Statute, Different Logic

The NPO corporation is governed by **a different statute family entirely** (NPO Act 1998), supervised by Prefectural Governors not by the Legal Affairs Bureau. Its key features:

- Permitted activities are restricted to **20 specified categories** (welfare, education, environment, international cooperation, disaster relief, community development, etc.). Activities outside these categories require a separate vehicle.
- Minimum **10 社 members** at establishment — higher than general-form associations.
- **No notary certification cost, no registration tax** — establishment cost is materially lower than for general forms.
- **Mandatory disclosure** — annual financial statements and activity reports must be publicly accessible at the Prefecture.
- Tax treatment: corporation tax only on collateral profit-making activities (similar to a public-interest foundation but narrower scope); donor deduction only for authorized-NPO corporations separately authorised by the NTA.

NPO is the right form for community-led, multi-stakeholder, civic-activity organisations. It is not a good form for endowment-driven research, large-scale grant-making, or activities outside the 20-category list.

## A Decision Tree

```
Q1. Is the organisation primarily endowment-driven or member-driven?
  Endowment → continue to Q2
  Members → continue to Q4

Q2. Will the activity match one of the 23 public-interest categories AND can the
    organisation pass the 50%-expenditure test from year 1?
  Yes → Public-Interest Foundation (start as a general foundation, apply for public-interest authorization)
  No, but planned long-term → General Foundation with public-interest plan
  No → General Foundation (general purpose)

Q3. Is the founder willing to dedicate ¥3M+ endowment at start?
  Yes → General / Public-Interest Foundation path
  No → cannot use foundation form; reconsider General Association or NPO

Q4. Does the activity fall within the 20 NPO Act categories?
  Yes, civic and public-disclosure-tolerant → NPO Corporation
  Yes, but want lighter ongoing disclosure → General Association
  No → General Association (or Public-Interest Association if public-interest authorization is feasible)
```

| Organ | Public-Interest Foundation | General Foundation | General Association | NPO Corporation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Council of councillors | Required | Required | N/A (no councillor concept) | N/A |
| Board of directors | Required (with public-interest-specific composition rules) | Required | Optional (a general association may operate without a board if very small) | Required |
| Auditors | Required | Required (1+) | Required only if board of directors is formed | Required (1+) |
| General meeting of members | N/A | N/A | Required (annual) | Required (annual) |

The cross-prohibition rules (councillors cannot serve as directors / auditors / employees of the same foundation; auditors cannot serve as directors / employees) apply to both the public-interest foundation and the general foundation — this is the **core independence architecture** of the foundation form. ## Tax Treatment in More Detail

The corporation-tax treatment is the highest-stakes practical difference between the four forms.

### Public-Interest Foundation / Public-Interest Association

- **Collateral profit-making business** is taxed at the standard corporation-tax rate
- **Public-interest activities** are excluded from corporation tax
- Income from securities portfolios, real-estate income from rental, etc., is in scope only if classified as collateral profit-making business under the enumerated 34 categories
- **Deemed donation deduction** allows a public-interest foundation to deduct funds transferred from collateral profit-making business to public-interest activities, up to specified ceilings

### General Foundation / General Association — two sub-types

- **Non-profit-type general corporation** — meets statutory non-profit requirements (no profit distribution clause, dissolution clause, board composition rules); only collateral profit-making business is taxed
- **Ordinary corporation treatment** — does not meet non-profit-type requirements; taxed broadly on all profit-making activity like a regular company

Most working general foundations / general associations elect non-profit-type treatment to limit corporation-tax exposure.

### NPO Corporation

- Specified non-profit activities (one of the 20 categories) excluded from corporation tax
- Collateral profit-making business taxed at standard rate
- Authorized-NPO status (separately granted by NTA) further unlocks donor-side deduction; without authorized status the entity itself is still tax-favoured but donors get no deduction

### Donor-side tax deduction — the asymmetric prize

The donor-side benefit is the unique public-interest / authorized-NPO advantage:

| Donor type | Public-Interest Foundation / Association | Authorized-NPO Corporation | General Foundation / Association | Non-authorized NPO Corporation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Individual income tax | Income deduction OR tax-credit deduction (choice) | Income deduction OR tax-credit deduction (choice) | No deduction | No deduction |
| Individual inheritance tax | Donation deductible from estate | Same | No | No |
| Corporate donor | Loss-deductible (expanded limit beyond general donation cap) | Same | General donation cap only | General donation cap only |
| Resident tax (individual) | Available where local government designates | Available where local government designates | No | No |

The expanded corporate-donor loss-deduction allowance is materially valuable for large corporate donors and is one of the strongest reasons mature corporate philanthropy vehicles convert from a general foundation to a public-interest foundation.

## Strategic reading

- For research-and-policy organisations starting from a settled endowment, the **staged path general foundation → public-interest foundation** is the textbook route. The intermediate general-foundation phase is not just bureaucratic — it is the de facto track-record period that Public-Interest Authorization Committee reviewers expect.
- For grant-receiving bodies, donor-deductibility under public-interest foundation status materially expands the addressable donor base. The implication is that public-interest authorization is not a vanity label but a fundraising-economics gate, especially for the research-grant ecosystem mapped in [[non-profit/japan-research-grant-map-2026|Japan research grant map 2026]].
- For activity-based, multi-stakeholder, community-facing organisations, the NPO corporation is the cheaper and culturally more familiar form, but is governed under a different statute family with stronger public-disclosure obligations than the general forms.
- Corporate philanthropic vehicles set up by listed groups often prefer the public-interest foundation because it allows the founder corporation to receive public credit while the foundation operates at arm's length under independent governance. This is the same separation logic used in mission-driven [[corporate-strategy/INDEX|corporate-strategy]] structures.
