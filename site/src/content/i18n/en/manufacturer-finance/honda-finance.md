## TL;DR

Honda's captive auto-finance centers on **American Honda Finance Corporation (AHFC) in the Americas**, **Honda Finance Co., Ltd. (Honda Finance) domestically**, and **Honda Canada Finance Inc. (HCFI) in Canada**, an auto captive second in scale to Toyota Financial Services. From a manufacturing standpoint, the axes worth reading are **(1) the significance of Honda Motor (Honda Giken Kogyo 7267) placing its finance in a consolidated subsidiary outside the parent; (2) the design that concentrates residual-value risk on the captive rather than the OEM parent; (3) the point that AHFC is a funding institution that regularly issues auto-loan and lease ABS in the US (connecting to [[structured-finance/auto-loan-abs-japan-toyota-honda|Japan / US auto-loan ABS]]); and (4) the parent-company liquidity-guarantee mechanism called a Keepwell Agreement**. AHFC provides retail finance under two brands (DBAs), HFS (Honda Financial Services) and AFS (Acura Financial Services), and together with Ford Motor Credit, Ally Financial, GM Financial, and Toyota Motor Credit forms the top tier of US auto captives.

### Relationship with Honda Motor parent

- Because AHFC and domestic Honda Finance are consolidated subsidiaries, they are disclosed on an aggregated basis as Honda Motor's **"Financial Services business" segment** in the securities reports and Form 20-F.
- In residual-value loans (residual-value credit) and leases, Honda Motor's parent **fixes the "new-car sales profit" first**, while the used-car price-fluctuation risk is concentrated on the captive's (AHFC / domestic Honda Finance) balance sheet. This is the same captive design as [[manufacturer-finance/toyota-financial-services|Toyota Financial Services]].
- A **Keepwell Agreement** is the mechanism by which, when AHFC raises funds via ABS or bonds, the parent HMC commits to "maintain AHFC's solvency and net worth." It is not legally a guarantee, but it has the effect of bringing AHFC's creditworthiness close to the parent's rating. It is the core of reducing the captive's funding cost.

### Connection to auto ABS

AHFC is one of the major ABS issuers among US auto captives, regularly securitizing auto-loan and lease receivables through the **Honda Auto Receivables Owner Trust** series. Domestic Honda Finance is also an issuer in Japan's auto-loan ABS market. The structure in which the captive moves off-balance-sheet, via ABS, the auto receivables that would otherwise pile up on Honda Motor's parent B/S, restraining the group's overall leverage, is common with the Toyota / Nissan camps. For details, see [[structured-finance/auto-loan-abs-japan-toyota-honda|Auto-loan ABS Japan (Toyota Finance, Honda Finance, Nissan Credit)]] and [[structured-finance/japan-auto-loan-abs-waterfall-mechanics|Japan auto-loan ABS waterfall mechanics]].

## 4. Strategy (Residual Value + EV + Brand-Split)

- **Residual-value risk management**: Residual-value credit and leases are directly tied to the used-car market price, and the technological renewal of BEVs (battery degradation, generational change) makes residual-value assessment difficult. The captive updates its residual-value model using used-car price data. For the general mechanism, see the residual-value section of [[manufacturer-finance/vendor-finance-mechanism|captive / vendor finance mechanism]].
- **Financial separation of the Acura brand**: For the luxury brand Acura, it sets up **Acura Financial Services (AFS)** as a DBA of AHFC, differentiating credit and product design from the Honda mass brand.
- **Scale of the US captive**: In the US market, it is on a scale comparable with Ford Motor Credit / Ally Financial and is a major issuer in the US auto-ABS market. That it discloses in detail via SEC registration and Form 10-K is also common with Toyota Motor Credit.
- **Emerging-market expansion**: Honda takes a strategy of establishing retail-finance subsidiaries in each country in tandem with its sales companies, expanding the sales-finance network for both four-wheel and two-wheel.
- **Captive in the EV / mobility era**: The trend of redesigning captive products to match the shift from ownership to usage (subscription, leasing) is in the same direction as Toyota's KINTO.

## 5. Regulation and policy

- **Domestic**: Financial Services Agency (FSA), the Money Lending Business Act, the Installment Sales Act, the Insurance Business Act. Domestic Honda Finance is under their supervision.
- **US**: the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), state financial supervision. AHFC bears continuous disclosure obligations as an SEC-registered issuer.
- **Canada**: federal and provincial financial supervision (HCFI).
- **Recent policy topics**:
  - 2024– transparency of EV residual-value assessment models (consumer protection)
  - 2025– the US CFPB's trend of strengthening APR disclosure in auto finance
  - 2024– personal-data protection in connected-car data × finance
  - trends in the ABS market (securitization regulation) for the captive's funding
