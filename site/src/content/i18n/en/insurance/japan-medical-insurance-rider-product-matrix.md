# Japan medical insurance rider product matrix

## TL;DR

Japanese medical insurance is a specialist franchise within the broader life-insurance industry. One foreign affiliate ([[JapanFG/aflac-japan|Aflac Japan]]) has historically held about 70% of the new cancer-insurance market, several other foreign affiliates ([[JapanFG/aia-life-japan|AIA Japan]], [[JapanFG/manulife-japan|Manulife Japan]], [[JapanFG/prudential-japan|Prudential Japan]]) run large medical / protection books, the life subsidiaries of the non-life big three ([[JapanFG/tokio-marine-nichido-life|Tokio Marine & Nichido Life]] / Anshin Seimei lineage, [[JapanFG/sompo-himawari-life|Sompo Himawari Life]], [[JapanFG/mitsui-sumitomo-aioi-life|Mitsui Sumitomo Aioi Life]]) compete for medical / rider share, and the life big four mutuals ([[JapanFG/nippon-life|Nippon Life]], [[JapanFG/dai-ichi-life|Dai-ichi Life]], [[JapanFG/sumitomo-life|Sumitomo Life]], [[JapanFG/meiji-yasuda|Meiji Yasuda]]) bundle medical riders into whole-life and term franchises. The product offering can be reduced to a small set of axes that determines competitive position: (1) primary line — cancer-specific (Aflac dominant), medical (per-day hospital benefit + surgery lump-sum), or rider (attached to a whole-life / term base policy); (2) premium-payment structure — whole-life payment, term payment, or short-payment-period (短期払) design; (3) benefit type — lump-sum (一時金), per-day hospital benefit (入院日額), expense reimbursement (実損填補), or combinations; (4) advanced-medical-treatment rider (先進医療) — almost universal but with cap differences; (5) channel mix — tied sales force, agency, bancassurance, or direct online; and (6) surrender-value design (解約返戻金) — savings style (with cash value) versus pure protection (no surrender value). The competitive heat map centers on cancer (Aflac vs Sompo Himawari vs AIA vs MS Aioi), pure medical (Aflac vs Sompo Himawari vs Tokio Marine Anshin vs Orix / Medicare-style players outside the matrix), and rider-attached medical (life big-4  vs foreign affiliates).

## Why this matrix matters

Reading a Japanese household's medical-coverage choice means choosing among at least three different product architectures bundled under the umbrella term "medical insurance" (医療保険):

1. **Cancer-specific (がん保険)** — a separate product line with separate underwriting, separate waiting periods, and separate premium curves. Aflac built the modern Japanese cancer-insurance market in the 1970s and has held a roughly 70% share of new business in the segment for decades.
2. **General medical (医療保険)** — per-day hospital benefit plus surgery lump-sum plus a rider menu (advanced medical, three major diseases, hospitalization lump-sum). Competition is hottest here.
3. **Rider attached to whole-life or term protection (医療特約 / 入院特約)** — bundled into a base life policy by the life big-4  mutuals and by foreign affiliates running protection-bundle plans (Sony Life lifeplanner, Prudential, Manulife).

The matrix below decomposes each axis. Specific product names, premium rates, channel commissions, and underwriting limits are date-specific and should be confirmed against each carrier's product brochure, FSA solicitation-rule disclosure, and annual / disclosure materials.

## Aflac Japan — cancer-specific dominance and modern medical menu

[[JapanFG/aflac-japan|Aflac Japan]] (Aflac Life Insurance Japan) is the historical and current leader in Japanese cancer insurance. Its structural features are a specialist cancer line (がん保険), broad agency and bancassurance distribution including a long-running Japan Post Bank tie-up, supplementary medical products (医療保険) that either bundle cancer benefits or stand alone, and a hybrid tied-sales-force / agency model. Aflac built the original Japanese cancer-insurance product in 1974 , and the modern menu is the most differentiated by riders (cancer-treatment lump-sum, hospitalization-per-day benefit during cancer treatment, advanced medical, outpatient cancer-treatment benefit). Premium payment is usually whole-life payment (終身払) or short-payment-period (短期払), with the choice changing the level-premium curve.

## AIA Japan — multi-line protection franchise

[[JapanFG/aia-life-japan|AIA Japan]] (AIA Group's Japanese subsidiary, inheriting the former AIG Star Life lineage) operates a full protection franchise across cancer, medical, term, whole life, and rider menus. AIA's competitive position in Japan combines agency distribution, partner-bank bancassurance, and a financial-adviser / lifeplanner-adjacent sales force. Its medical / cancer book is a major contributor to new business value.

## Tokio Marine Anshin lineage — life affiliate of a non-life big-3  group

[[JapanFG/tokio-marine-nichido-life|Tokio Marine & Nichido Life]] (the Anshin Seimei legacy) is the life affiliate of the [[JapanFG/tokio-marine|Tokio Marine Holdings]] non-life group. The medical / cancer franchise was historically centered on the “Medical Kit R” series and successor product families, which competed directly with Aflac in medical and with Sompo Himawari in cross-line distribution. The parent non-life group's nationwide agency network is the structural distribution advantage.

## Sompo Himawari Life — life affiliate of a non-life big-3  group

[[JapanFG/sompo-himawari-life|Sompo Himawari Life]] (former Sompo Japan Nipponkoa Himawari Life; now Sompo Himawari Life) is the life subsidiary of [[JapanFG/sompo|Sompo Holdings]]. Its structural strength in medical lies in a strong rider-product franchise spanning cancer, three-major-disease cover, women-specific medical, and advanced-medical riders. Distribution combines tied sales force, agency, and partner-bank channels.

## Mitsui Sumitomo Aioi Life — MS&AD life affiliate

[[JapanFG/mitsui-sumitomo-aioi-life|Mitsui Sumitomo Aioi Life]] is the life subsidiary of the [[JapanFG/msad|MS&AD]] group. Its medical / rider franchise sits inside the MS&AD distribution ecosystem, with access to both [[JapanFG/mitsui-sumitomo-insurance|MSI]] and [[JapanFG/aioi-nissay-dowa-insurance|ADI]] agency networks. The product menu covers medical, cancer riders, term, whole life, and individual annuities.

## Foreign affiliates beyond Aflac and AIA — Manulife, Prudential, MetLife, AXA

[[JapanFG/manulife-japan|Manulife Japan]] and [[JapanFG/prudential-japan|Prudential Japan]] operate protection-led franchises with lifeplanner / FA-style sales forces. Their medical / rider menus are anchored more by whole-life and term protection with medical riders than by stand-alone medical or cancer products. [[JapanFG/axa-japan|AXA Japan]] (former AXA Life Japan) runs a multi-channel medical / protection franchise. MetLife Japan, AIG Life Japan, and smaller foreign affiliates round out the competitive set.

## Life big-4  mutuals — bundled medical riders inside whole-life policies

The four big mutuals — [[JapanFG/nippon-life|Nippon Life]], [[JapanFG/dai-ichi-life|Dai-ichi Life]], [[JapanFG/sumitomo-life|Sumitomo Life]], [[JapanFG/meiji-yasuda|Meiji Yasuda]] — distribute medical coverage mainly as riders attached to whole-life or term base policies through their tied sales forces. Sumitomo Life's Vitality-branded wellness-linked product is the most differentiated structural feature among the four; the others compete on rider-menu breadth, sales-force trust, and corporate-group cross-sell. Each also runs stand-alone medical / cancer products through agency and bancassurance channels for customers who prefer specialist coverage.

## Historical and structural context

The current Japanese medical-insurance structure reflects several decades of product and regulatory evolution:

- **1974  Aflac's entry into cancer insurance** introduced specialist cancer insurance to the Japanese market and built the segment. Aflac's distribution combined corporate / employer-group sales and agency channels.
- **1995  Revision of the Insurance Business Act** modernized the licensing regime and laid the foundation for expansion by foreign affiliates in protection / medical specialty lines.
- **1996–2001  third-sector liberalization** opened the medical / cancer / nursing-care specialty to foreign affiliates and to the life subsidiaries of non-life groups on a level playing field. The third sector (第三分野) is the regulatory category for medical / cancer / nursing products between life insurance (第一分野) and non-life insurance (第二分野).
- **Japan Post Bank distribution alliance from 2001  onward** scaled Aflac's cancer-insurance reach through Japan Post Bank's counter network.
- **Standardization of the advanced-medical rider from the late 2000s onward** made the advanced-medical-treatment rider an industry-standard add-on with high actual-cost caps.
- **The short-payment-period product wave in the 2010s** offered 60-payment / 65-payment level-premium structures that finish premium payment before retirement while whole-life coverage continues.
- **Foreign-currency bundle expansion in the 2010s** combined foreign-currency whole-life with medical riders through bancassurance, distributed by [[JapanFG/dai-ichi-frontier-life|Dai-ichi Frontier Life]], [[JapanFG/ms-primary-life|MS Primary Life]], Manulife, and similar players.
- **Expansion of limited-underwriting products (引受基準緩和) from 2015  onward** opened medical / cancer coverage to older or mildly impaired applicants who would have been declined under standard underwriting.
- **Wellness-linked products in the 2020s** ([[JapanFG/sumitomo-life|Sumitomo Life]] Vitality and similar) tie premium discounts to health behavior and biometric data.
- **ESR rollout in 2025 ** under the FSA's economic-value-based solvency regime affects the capital treatment of medical / cancer liability cash flows for all FSA-perimeter carriers — see [[insurance/economic-value-based-solvency|economic-value-based solvency]] and [[insurance/esr-economic-value-solvency|ESR]].

## Decision use

Use this matrix when reading the Japanese medical-insurance competitive set against each other rather than against the broader life industry. Practical questions:

- **Cancer specialty vs medical bundle.** Aflac dominates stand-alone cancer; multiple carriers compete on medical. A household buying cancer-specific cover faces a different competitive set than one buying general medical.
- **Surrender-value design.** Modern medical / cancer products are designed as pure protection with no meaningful surrender value. Older policies may include surrender value. The choice affects the premium curve and tax treatment.
- **Whole life vs short payment.** Short-payment-period products finish premium payment before retirement at the cost of higher early-year premiums. The path of surrender value (解約返戻金) and the economics of protection cost differ.
- **Foreign-currency medical bundle.** USD-denominated whole-life with a medical rider transfers FX risk to the policyholder but offers a higher cash-value projection grammar. Bancassurance is the principal distribution route.
- **Channel suitability.** Tied sales force is the dominant channel for medical riders sold by life big-4 ; agency / shop is the dominant channel for stand-alone medical / cancer sold by foreign affiliates; bancassurance dominates foreign-currency bundles; online direct is the [[JapanFG/lifenet|Lifenet]] / [[JapanFG/sbi-life|SBI Life]] lane.
- **Kyosai-perimeter alternative.** Many households hold both FSA-perimeter medical / cancer coverage and kyosai medical riders (JA Kyosai / Zenrosai / Kenmin Kyosai / COOP Kyosai). See [[insurance/japan-kyosai-vs-fsa-insurance-perimeter-matrix|the kyosai vs FSA perimeter matrix]] for the cooperative-perimeter overlay.
- **MHLW national-health-insurance interaction.** Japanese medical-insurance products sit on top of [[insurance/medical-cancer-insurance-product-economics|public health insurance system]] (public health insurance), where MHLW caps out-of-pocket costs through the high-cost medical treatment system (高額療養費). Supplementary-cover products assume this base layer.

## Boundary cases / caveats

- **Numbers are conceptual.** Market shares (especially Aflac's ~70% cancer share), premium rates, benefit caps, and rider availability change with each product cycle and disclosure period. Sources are the FSA, Seiho industry materials, each carrier's product brochures and integrated / disclosure reports, and MHLW publications on public health insurance.
- **Aflac cancer share.** The ~70% figure is widely used as industry shorthand for new cancer-insurance market share and has held for many years. Specific recent vintages should be confirmed against Aflac disclosure and Seiho industry data.
- **Carrier naming history.** Several carriers have been renamed or merged (Anshin Seimei -> Tokio Marine & Nichido Life lineage; Sompo Japan Nipponkoa Himawari Life -> Sompo Himawari Life; AIG Star Life -> AIA Japan lineage; Sumitomo Life Vitality is a wellness-linked product line, not a separate carrier). The matrix uses current entity names.
- **Third sector (第三分野).** Medical, cancer, and nursing products sit in the third sector for regulatory purposes — between life insurance (第一分野) and non-life insurance (第二分野). All FSA-perimeter life carriers (and non-life carriers via life subsidiaries) participate.
- **Foreign-currency products.** Foreign-currency whole-life with a medical rider transfers FX risk to the policyholder. Conduct rules require dedicated suitability and explanation for these products, especially for elderly customers. See [[insurance/bancassurance-economics-japan|bancassurance economics Japan]].
- **Online-direct contrast.** [[JapanFG/lifenet|Lifenet]] and [[JapanFG/sbi-life|SBI Life]] do not appear as primary competitors in the cancer-specialty matrix above, but they are growing players in pure medical / cancer specialty at lower premium points. See [[insurance/internet-life-insurance-business-model|the internet life model]].
- **Wellness-linked products.** Vitality and similar wellness-linked menus reward behavior with premium discounts. This structural feature affects long-run pricing and persistency but does not change product-line classification.
- **Surrender value and tax.** Surrender value (解約返戻金) on medical / cancer products is generally suppressed by design; tax treatment of cash value on whole-life base policies follows the standard income-tax code.
- **Public health insurance base.** Japanese supplementary medical products assume the public-health-insurance base. Changes in the high-cost medical treatment cap rules (高額療養費) can affect supplementary-product economics.
- **Kampo Life product menu.** Subject to special-law approval constraints. Read [[insurance/kampo-japan-post-insurance|the Kampo entry]] separately.
