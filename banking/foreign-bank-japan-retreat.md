# Foreign-bank Japan retreat pattern


## Wiki route

This entry sits under [[banking/INDEX|banking index]]. Read it against [[banking/post-megabank-positioning|Post-megabank positioning]] for peer / contrast context and [[banking/cooperative-banking-japan|Cooperative banking in Japan]] for the broader system / regulatory boundary.

## Overview

The useful pattern is not "foreign banks left Japan." FSA's current foreign-bank branch list still shows many licensed foreign bank branches. The real pattern is that many foreign banks have narrowed Japan from broad retail / mass-market ambitions to wholesale, institutional, custody, markets, corporate banking, or representative / branch functions where Japan-specific scale economics make sense.

## Why Retail Is Hard

Foreign banks face several structural disadvantages in Japanese retail banking:

- megabanks, regional banks, Japan Post Bank, and net banks already cover most retail needs;
- trust and brand familiarity are local;
- branch networks and cash / ATM partnerships are expensive;
- low-rate deposit economics historically made retail deposits less attractive;
- consumer compliance, AML, language, and operations require Japan-specific scale.

Wholesale and institutional banking can still work because the customer base is narrower, cross-border capabilities matter, and global balance-sheet / product expertise is valuable.

## Regulatory Reading

Foreign bank branches are supervised under the Banking Act and FSA's major-bank supervision guideline. The foreign-branch section emphasizes branch governance, home-office control, domestic asset / funding behavior, internal controls, and compliance with Japanese laws and rules.

Do not infer "unlicensed" from a brand's reduced retail presence. Check the current FSA foreign-bank branch list and the specific entity's Japan disclosure.
